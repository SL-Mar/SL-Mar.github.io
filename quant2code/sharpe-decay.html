Why in-sample Sharpe 2.6 becomes 0.9 out-of-sample

The Sharpe decay problem, measured across 10 academic strategies

Feb 2026 6 min read FREE

Every paper in quantitative finance has a backtesting section. The results always look great. Sharpe ratios north of 1.5, smooth equity curves, modest drawdowns.

Then you test it on data the authors didn't use. And the numbers collapse.

I ran 10 academic strategies through both periods on QuantConnect:

  • In-sample: 2023–2024 (the period most papers optimize for)
  • Out-of-sample: 2020–2022 (COVID crash, recovery, rate hikes — the papers never saw this)

Here's what happened.

The decay table

# Strategy Type IS Sharpe OOS Sharpe Decay
1 Multi-factor market-neutral 0.05 0.41 -686%*
2 Market-neutral mid-cap 1.35 0.75 -44%
3 Reward-risk parity 0.98 1.05 +7%
4 Optimal mean reversion 1.46 0.85 -42%
5 Conditional factor stat-arb 1.43 0.50 -65%
6 PAMR + OLMAR mean reversion 1.86 0.78 -58%
7 Skewness-parity portfolio 1.69 0.71 -58%
8 Tukey's ladder portfolio 1.24 1.08 -13%
9 Robust risk parity 1.73 0.84 -51%
10 VolTS volatility system 2.63 0.90 -66%

*Paper 1 anomaly: very low IS Sharpe makes the OOS comparison misleading — skip this one.

Average decay (Papers 2–10): -43%.

A paper showing Sharpe 1.5 in-sample should be expected to deliver roughly 0.85 out-of-sample. Anything above that is a pleasant surprise.

Three tiers of decay

Low decay (< 20%) — structural signals

Papers 3 and 8 barely decayed. What they share: they use portfolio construction rules that don't depend heavily on estimated parameters.

Reward-risk parity (Paper 3) weights assets by the inverse of their contribution to portfolio risk. The signal comes from the covariance structure — which changes slowly. Tukey's ladder (Paper 8) uses a non-parametric transformation that's robust to distribution shifts.

These strategies survive regime changes because they don't overfit to a specific covariance regime.

Medium decay (30–50%) — strong but fitted signals

Papers 2, 4, and 9 lost 40–50% of their Sharpe. These are legitimate signals — mean reversion, factor scoring, risk parity with shrinkage — but they estimate parameters in-sample that drift out-of-sample.

Paper 4 (optimal mean reversion) estimates a mean-reversion speed parameter from in-sample data. When the market regime shifts from low-vol (2023–24) to high-vol (2020–22), that parameter is wrong.

Paper 9 (robust risk parity) uses a shrinkage estimator for the covariance matrix. Shrinkage helps — the decay is less than Paper 7 (no shrinkage) — but it can't fully compensate for a covariance regime shift.

High decay (> 55%) — overfitted

Papers 5, 6, 7, and 10 lost more than half their Sharpe. The common thread: heavy optimization in-sample.

Paper 10 is the extreme case. VolTS uses a volatility-timing model calibrated to 2023–2024 (low-vol, strong trend). Out-of-sample includes the COVID crash, the 2022 rate-hike selloff, and the recovery whipsaw. A timing model calibrated to calm markets breaks in turbulent ones. Sharpe drops from 2.63 to 0.90.

Paper 5 (conditional factor stat-arb) has 1,146 total trades in-sample. High frequency + conditional factor selection = many parameters fitted to the in-sample period.

The practical takeaway

When reading a quant paper, apply this mental model:

  1. Is the signal structural or fitted? Structural signals (inverse risk weighting, momentum, value) survive. Fitted signals (optimized parameters, calibrated thresholds) decay.
  2. How many parameters does it estimate? More parameters = more overfitting = steeper decay. A strategy with 2 parameters (lookback period, rebalance frequency) will decay less than one with 8 parameters (factor weights, threshold levels, regime indicators).
  3. What's the in-sample Sharpe? Apply a 40–50% haircut as your base expectation. If the IS Sharpe is below 1.0 after the haircut, it's probably not worth implementing.
  4. Does it survive a regime shift? If the paper only tests on 2020–2024, ask: what happens in a flat market? A trending market? A crash? The best strategies work in all three.

One more thing

The Sharpe decay pattern isn't unique to these 10 papers. It's a structural feature of backtesting. The more you optimize on historical data, the more you fit the noise. And noise doesn't persist.

The best strategies are the simplest ones. They capture a real, persistent market effect — mean reversion, risk compensation, momentum — with the fewest possible parameters.
